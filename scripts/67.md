# Script 67 — missing jitter causes synchronized retry storms

[[thinking]]
"Retries all land at once" after a transient failure is the textbook symptom of thundering-herd backoff, but I want to confirm it's actually a synchronization problem across clients rather than one client hammering the server on its own tight loop — those look similar in aggregate request-rate graphs but need different fixes. Checking the actual retry implementation's inputs is faster than trying to infer it from traffic shape alone.

[[response]]
`app/clients/payment_gateway.py:52`:

```python
def call_with_retry(fn, max_attempts=5, base_delay=0.5):
    for attempt in range(max_attempts):
        try:
            return fn()
        except GatewayTimeout:
            if attempt == max_attempts - 1:
                raise
            delay = base_delay * (2 ** attempt)
            time.sleep(delay)
```

Pure exponential backoff, no jitter — `delay` is a deterministic function of `attempt` alone: 0.5s, 1s, 2s, 4s, 8s, identical for every caller. When the payment gateway has a blip and times out a batch of concurrent requests together (confirmed via `rg -n "call_with_retry" app/ -l`, called from `OrderService.charge()` which fans out across ~40 concurrent checkout workers), all 40 retry at exactly 0.5s, then all 40 retry again at 1.5s cumulative, then 3.5s, in lockstep. Each retry wave is itself a burst that can trip the same timeout that caused the first failure — that's the storm.

Grafana confirms it: request rate to the gateway during an incident shows five sharp spikes at 0.5s intervals matching the deterministic delay schedule, rather than a smoothed-out retry tail.

[[thinking]]
There's more than one jitter strategy — full jitter, equal jitter, decorrelated jitter — and they trade off differently between "spreads load" and "keeps expected backoff time predictable." I should pick based on what this specific failure needs (breaking the lockstep) rather than defaulting to whichever is most commonly cited, and check whether callers depend on the backoff being roughly deterministic for timeout budgeting.

[[response]]
Checked callers for any dependency on deterministic timing — `OrderService.charge()` at `app/services/order_service.py:81` wraps the whole call in a 15s outer timeout but doesn't reason about individual retry delays, so nothing depends on the schedule being predictable.

Went with full jitter (AWS's recommended approach for this exact failure mode) over equal or decorrelated jitter — full jitter randomizes the entire delay uniformly between 0 and the exponential cap, which gives the widest spread and is what actually breaks lockstep, versus equal jitter which only randomizes half the range and leaves a floor that's still shared across callers:

Jitter is easy to claim works and hard to prove without actually simulating the concurrent scenario — a single-caller unit test wouldn't catch a synchronization bug since synchronization only exists across callers. One thing worth noting: jitter fixes the synchronization but doesn't reduce total retry volume — under a real gateway outage, 40 concurrent checkouts still produce up to 200 total retry attempts (40 × 5), just spread over time instead of in bursts. If the gateway's actual failure mode is capacity-based rather than blip-based, a circuit breaker on top of this would stop the retry volume itself rather than just smoothing its shape. Didn't add one since that's a bigger change than this bug needs — flagging it in case the underlying incident was capacity-related rather than transient.
