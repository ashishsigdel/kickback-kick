[[thinking]]
Reviewing a design is different from reviewing code: the cost of a wrong call is higher because it's public surface you can't quietly refactor later. So I want to separate what's genuinely irreversible — URL shape, status codes, pagination contract — from what's a preference I could live with either way.

[[response]]
Read the proposal. The resource modelling is good — nouns not verbs, consistent nesting, no RPC-shaped endpoints leaking through. Three things I'd change, and only one of them is urgent.

**1. Pagination is offset-based** — `api/v2/orders.py:22`

```
GET /v2/orders?offset=200&limit=50
```

Offset pagination drifts when rows are inserted mid-scan: a client paging through gets duplicates and misses. On an orders endpoint, where inserts are constant, that's not theoretical.

Cursor pagination is the fix, and it has to happen before launch — it's the one change here that's genuinely hard to make later, because clients will have built against `offset`.

```
GET /v2/orders?after=eyJpZCI6MjAwfQ&limit=50
```

**2. `PATCH` returns 200 with an empty body** — `api/v2/orders.py:71`

Either return the updated resource with 200, or return 204 with no body. An empty 200 makes clients guess whether to re-fetch.

**3. Errors are strings** — `api/v2/errors.py:14`

`{"error": "invalid customer"}` can't be handled programmatically. A stable `code` field alongside the human message costs nothing now and is a breaking change later.

Let me look at how versioning is handled before I say anything stronger, because that determines how expensive these are to fix after the fact.

[[thinking]]
This is the crux. If they have a real versioning story, then only pagination is urgent and the rest can land in v3. If /v2 is the whole plan and there's no path to v3, then all three become launch blockers because there's no cheap way to change any of them later.

#3 drops down: adding a `code` field to an existing error object doesn't break a client that ignores unknown fields.

[[response]]
**Summary**

Two blockers before launch: cursor pagination (#1) and the PATCH response contract (#2). Both are cheap now and expensive the day after you have external clients.

The larger finding is the one that isn't on the list: **there's no deprecation or versioning policy**, and that's what turns two small fixes into blockers. With a documented policy — even something as simple as "URL-versioned, previous version supported for 6 months" — none of these would be launch-blocking, because you'd have a route to change them.

I'd suggest, in order:

1. Cursor pagination on `/v2/orders` — the only genuinely irreversible one.
2. Pick 204 or return-the-resource for PATCH, consistently across all mutating endpoints.
3. Write down the versioning policy, even briefly. It's a paragraph, and it's what makes every future decision here reversible.

I haven't changed any code — this is a design review, and #1 in particular is a decision about your client contract that shouldn't be made by whoever happened to read the file. Happy to implement the cursor pagination if you want it, but that's your call to make first.
